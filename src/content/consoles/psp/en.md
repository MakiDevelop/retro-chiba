On December 12, 2004, Sony launched the PlayStation Portable (PSP) in Japan at ¥20,790. **It was Sony's first handheld** — the first time since the 1989 Game Boy that Nintendo's monopoly on the dedicated handheld market faced a frontal challenge from a rival with home-console pedigree. The architect was **Ken Kutaragi** (the father of PS1/PS2, simultaneously leading PS3 development), and the PSP's brief was unambiguous: **fit PS2-class 3D graphics into a pocket**.

On paper the PSP comprehensively outclassed the contemporaneous Nintendo DS. **A MIPS R4000-derivative CPU at 222–333 MHz** (vs the DS's 67 MHz ARM), a **4.3-inch widescreen 480×272 TFT-LCD** (vs the DS's two 256×192 panels), **32–64 MB of RAM** (vs the DS's 4 MB), PS2-class 3D graphics (the DS managed only 2D plus simplified 3D), built-in 802.11b Wi-Fi, and stereo speakers. **The PSP was, in spec terms, a handheld-scale PS2** — yet Nintendo's interface-driven differentiation (dual screens, touch, microphone) sidestepped that arms race entirely, which is the central reason the DS sold 154M to the PSP's 80M.

The most distinctive hardware decision was **UMD (Universal Media Disc) optical media** — a 6 cm, 1.8 GB mini-disc. **It remains the only time in handheld history that a portable game system shipped with optical media** (every prior handheld used cartridges). UMD also encoded Sony's parallel ambition to position the PSP as a **personal media player**: feature films were sold on UMD, and Sony Pictures pushed releases like *Spider-Man* into the format. **The UMD movie business was effectively dead by 2007** — nobody wanted to buy films exclusively for a PSP-sized screen — and UMD's slow load times and battery drain pushed the player community toward Memory Stick ROM dumps for actual play.

**The PSP's defining Asian social phenomenon was *Monster Hunter Portable 2nd G*** (Capcom, 2008; localized as *Monster Hunter Freedom Unite*) — a cooperative hunting RPG that used the PSP's Ad-hoc Wi-Fi to link four players in the same room. **Family restaurants across Japan — Denny's, Gusto, Saizeriya — turned into hunting halls**: students and salarymen sat at a single table for four to six hours playing Monster Hunter, a habit that defined 2008–2012. **Taiwan, Hong Kong, and mainland China followed**, with internet cafés, McDonald's, and casual restaurants becoming the analogous Sino-sphere venues — for that generation of Chinese-speaking players, "PSP at the table" is a core memory of the late-2000s.

Software-wise the PSP carried an unusually deep third-party catalog: **Monster Hunter** (Capcom), **Crisis Core: Final Fantasy VII** (Square Enix, 2007 — the FFVII prequel), **Persona 3 Portable** (Atlus, 2009 — the female-protagonist re-release), **GTA: Liberty City Stories / Vice City Stories** (Rockstar, full PS2-era GTAs adapted to a handheld), **Lumines** (Q Entertainment / Tetsuya Mizuguchi — synesthetic music puzzler), and **Final Fantasy Tactics: The War of the Lions** (Square Enix, 2007).

For Chinese-speaking players, the PSP is also **the largest handheld piracy and custom-firmware ecosystem in handheld history** — the PSP-1000 and 2000 were both fully exploitable through CFW (Custom Firmware), and **a single 16–32 GB Memory Stick Pro Duo could hold fifty to a hundred games**. **The "PSP + CFW + bulk ROM library" stack was the standard configuration for mainland Chinese middle-school students from 2007–2012**, the channel through which most of that cohort first encountered Monster Hunter, Final Fantasy, and Persona. Nintendo's R4-flashed DS and Sony's CFW-modded PSP were the two parallel gateways into Japanese RPGs for an entire generation of Sino-sphere players.

The PSP shipped in five major SKUs: **PSP-1000** (2004, original, heavier) → **PSP-2000 Slim & Lite** (2007, slimmer, TV-out added) → **PSP-3000** (2008, brighter screen, hardened against piracy) → **PSP Go** (2009, download-only sliding form factor — a commercial failure) → **PSP E-1000 / Street** (2011, stripped-down, Wi-Fi-less budget revision). **The Go was Sony's failed attempt to bypass UMD entirely**: retailers protested (no physical inventory to sell), customers protested (no UMD upgrade path), and the SKU sold poorly.

Commercially, PSP cleared **80M+ units globally** (Sony has never released a precise lifetime number) — **far short of the DS's 154M, but the single instance in which Sony genuinely dented Nintendo's hold on the dedicated handheld market**. The successor PS Vita was crushed between the DS/3DS and smartphones, collapsing to roughly 15M units. **PSP was the high-water mark — and the final high-water mark — of Sony's handheld business**.
